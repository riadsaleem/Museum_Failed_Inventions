About Our Museum

Celebrating the courage to innovate, even when it spectacularly fails

Our Mission

The Museum of Failed Inventions exists to honor the bold spirit of innovation, even when it doesn't go according to plan. We believe that failure is not the opposite of success, but a stepping stone to it. Every spectacular failure teaches us something valuable about human nature, market dynamics, and the unpredictable path of progress.

Our collection showcases the most ambitious, bizarre, and hilariously misguided inventions throughout history. From corporate giants' multi-million dollar mistakes to individual inventors' quirky creations, we celebrate the courage it takes to try something new, even when the world isn't ready for it.

Innovation Never Stops

Even in failure, there's always something to learn

Forgotten Inventors

Meet the brilliant minds behind history's most spectacular failures

Roy J. Plunkett

DuPont Chemist

While trying to create a new refrigerant in 1938, Plunkett accidentally discovered Teflon. What seemed like a failed experiment became one of the most useful materials ever invented. Sometimes the best discoveries come from the most unexpected failures.

Accidental Success

John Purves

British Inventor

Created the Dynasphere in the 1930s, a monowheel vehicle inspired by Leonardo da Vinci's designs. While it could reach 30 mph, the risk of "gerbiling" (tumbling inside the wheel) made it impractical. A brilliant concept ahead of its time.

Visionary Failure

Sergey Brin & Larry Page

Google Co-founders

The masterminds behind Google Glass, a revolutionary concept that was simply too early for its time. Privacy concerns and social stigma killed the consumer version, but the technology lives on in enterprise applications.

Too Early

Dr. Emma Read

London Pediatrician

Promoted the use of "baby cages" in 1930s London to give urban infants fresh air. While well-intentioned from a health perspective, the obvious safety risks made this one of history's most questionable parenting innovations.

Good Intentions

Charles Steinlauf

Bicycle Innovator

Designed the "family bicycle" that could accommodate multiple riders and even included a sewing machine attachment. While impractical, it showed remarkable creativity in trying to make cycling a complete family activity.

Creative Genius

Anonymous Inventor

1960s Innovator

Created the massage helmet for at-home scalp treatments. While the concept was sound, the bulky design and limited effectiveness meant it never caught on. Modern versions of this idea are still being developed today.

Ahead of Time

Our Philosophy

"Success is going from failure to failure without losing your enthusiasm."
- Winston Churchill

We believe that every failed invention represents someone's dream, someone's vision of a better world. While these inventions may not have succeeded commercially, they represent the human spirit of innovation and the courage to think differently.

In a world that often only celebrates success, we choose to honor the attempts, the experiments, and the bold ideas that didn't quite work out. Because without these "failures," we would never have the successes we celebrate today.
